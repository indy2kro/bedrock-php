# Bedrock PHP — v1.0

## 11. When to Leave Bedrock PHP

### Normative

### Principle
A standard is successful only while its constraints reduce risk and decision-making. When constraints become a liability, the standard should be exited deliberately.

---

### Rule 1: Exit Is a Valid Outcome

#### Rule
Leaving Bedrock PHP is an acceptable and expected outcome for some systems.

#### Implications
- Exit is not treated as failure.
- Exit decisions are documented and intentional.

#### Prohibitions
- No shaming or stigma around exiting.
- No indefinite limbo states.

#### Stop rule
If the team feels compelled to justify staying rather than leaving, reassess fit.

---

### Rule 2: Repeated Deviations Trigger Exit

#### Rule
If multiple core chapters require permanent deviations, Bedrock PHP is no longer a good fit.

#### Implications
- Deviations are tracked centrally.
- Patterns of deviation are reviewed periodically.

#### Prohibitions
- No normalizing constant exceptions.
- No redefining rules informally.

#### Stop rule
If deviations span multiple chapters, begin exit planning.

---

### Rule 3: Certain Constraints Override Bedrock PHP

#### Rule
Some constraints supersede Bedrock PHP by necessity.

Valid examples include:
- regulatory or compliance requirements
- organizational scale requiring independent team autonomy
- availability or latency requirements exceeding simple architectures

#### Implications
- Bedrock PHP does not compete with hard constraints.
- The system evolves to meet reality.

#### Prohibitions
- No forcing Bedrock PHP where it clearly does not fit.

#### Stop rule
If Bedrock PHP blocks required outcomes, exit.

---

### Rule 4: Exit Must Be Explicit and Documented

#### Rule
Exiting Bedrock PHP requires a written record.

#### Implications
- Exit date is recorded.
- Reasons are stated.
- Replacement approach is named.

#### Prohibitions
- No silent exits.
- No continuing to claim compliance after exit.

#### Stop rule
If the system no longer follows the rules, stop using the label immediately.

---

### Non-Normative

#### Why defining exits matters
Most standards fail quietly.

Teams drift away while:
- still using the language
- still claiming compliance
- no longer following the rules

This creates confusion and false confidence.

Explicit exits preserve honesty.

---

#### Bedrock PHP optimizes for a phase
Bedrock PHP is strongest when:

- teams are small to mid-sized
- systems are long-lived but not hyper-scale
- operational simplicity is a competitive advantage

As systems grow:
- coordination costs increase
- specialization emerges
- constraints change

Outgrowing the standard is healthy.

---

#### Common exit signals
Signals that it may be time to leave:

- frequent exceptions to deployment rules
- persistent pressure for multiple runtimes or pipelines
- operational needs that demand complex orchestration
- repeated arguments against core defaults

These are not failures — they are signals.

---

#### Exiting cleanly preserves trust
A clean exit:

- aligns expectations
- avoids half-adopted rules
- clarifies new responsibilities

It is better to leave cleanly than to stay dishonestly.

---

#### Example exit note

> We are exiting Bedrock PHP v1.0 as of 2026-03-01.
> Primary drivers: regulatory requirements and multi-team autonomy.
> Deviations required: multiple deployment pipelines, custom auth controls.
> Replacement approach: internal platform standard v3.

This level of clarity is sufficient.

---

#### Things to watch for
Unhealthy exits often look like:

- rules quietly ignored
- language retained without meaning
- new complexity layered without acknowledgment

These erode trust in standards overall.
